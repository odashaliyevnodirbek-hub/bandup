📖 Reading Strategy
How to Tackle True / False / Not Given Questions
✍️ BandUp Team ⏱ 5 min read 📅 February 2026 🎓 All levels

True / False / Not Given (T/F/NG) questions are among the most challenging question types in IELTS Reading. Many students confuse FALSE with NOT GIVEN — and this single mistake can cost several band points. In this article, we will break down the exact strategy you need to answer these questions correctly every time.

Understanding the Difference

The key to mastering T/F/NG questions is understanding what each answer actually means. Many students treat FALSE and NOT GIVEN as similar — but they are fundamentally different.

TRUE means the statement corresponds with information in the passage. The passage directly confirms what the statement says.

FALSE means the statement contradicts information in the passage. The passage says the opposite of what the statement claims.

NOT GIVEN means the information in the statement is simply not mentioned in the passage. It is neither confirmed nor contradicted. The passage is silent on the topic.

The Most Common Mistake

The most prevalent mistake students make is choosing FALSE when the correct answer is NOT GIVEN. This happens because students assume that if something is not mentioned, it must be wrong. This assumption is incorrect. If the passage does not address the topic at all, the answer is always NOT GIVEN — regardless of what you personally know or believe.

A Step-by-Step Strategy

Step 1 — Read the statement carefully. Identify the key claim being made. What is the statement actually saying?

Step 2 — Locate the relevant section. Find the part of the passage that discusses the same topic as the statement. Use keywords to scan efficiently.

Step 3 — Compare carefully. Read that section of the passage and compare it to the statement word by word. Does the passage confirm the statement? Does it say something different? Or does it not mention the topic at all?

Step 4 — Apply the rule. If the passage agrees → TRUE. If the passage says the opposite → FALSE. If the passage does not mention it → NOT GIVEN.

Practice Example

Consider this statement: "The Cadbury family paid their workers higher wages than other manufacturers."

Looking at the Chocolate for the Masses passage, we can see that the passage mentions the Cadburys created good housing and facilities for workers — but it says nothing about their wages specifically. The answer is therefore NOT GIVEN — the passage neither confirms nor denies this claim.

Key Takeaway

Always base your answers exclusively on what is written in the passage — never on your own knowledge or assumptions. This single principle, applied consistently, will dramatically improve your reading score.
